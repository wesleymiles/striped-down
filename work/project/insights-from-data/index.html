Best practices

Sometimes the practice breaks a convention enough to retire it. I consider these a type of data because it’s a step toward looking at designs objectively.

Cross-site changes

For both of these, the team found that our existing practices were outdated, and we implemented alternatives accordingly.

  1. Stopped using quicklink flippers

    Upon research, we learned a method of button flippers we used didn’t follow industry best practices. These multi-state elements turnover to display new info on their back. What was wrong with them?

    • They hide conversion opportunities.
    • They require a mouse-hover on desktop, but on touchscreens, they lack an indication they’re flippable. So people get surprised; which makes them feel less in control. A no-no.
    • Clients liked seeing the flipping animation more they considered what links went on the back. Some instances wound up with a dump of links which didn’t consider the user.

    What I did

    • Project lead
    • Data gathering
    • Wrote documentation

  2. Removed Form Field Box Titles from field

    Putting field titles where placeholder text should go does save vertical space and reduces some clutter, but we decided the cons outweigh the aesthetic.

    Cons: As the viewer begins to type their responses in the form field, the prompt disappears. When submitting the form, if there was an error the title would no longer be present which may confuse someone. This may deter the user from submitting the form.

    What I did

    • Project lead
    • Data gathering
    • Wrote documentation

    Field labels should go above not within fields.

Analytics Research

No special setup for this data, just knowing where to look and how to organize it. Two projects outlined:

Browser Support

Looked at a year of data for our top traffic clients to determine what percentage of traffic was coming from various browsers.

Result: Stopped support for older browsers which freed up dev time

We determined to fully support browsers which contribute 1%+ of traffic to our top sites. Decided to support browsers that provide .05%-1% on an as requested basis. Stopped supporting browsers that provide less than .04% of traffic.

Promotion conversion

Featuring discounts and sales are a big part of our client's marketing. They often ask us what ones to choose. We had some insight, but didn't align as a company. To get some data, we:

  • Researched a years worth of data for 40 clients
  • Categorized promotions top 11 promotions types
  • Calculated the direct conversion rate based page views / phone calls made from that page.

Result: a list of the highest-converting promo types (and ones not to use)

This research lead to PDFs to help clients choose what to promote and to the company being the expert in this area.

AB Testing

Our CRO team answered questions as they came in. We vetted many platforms but landed on Google Optimize.

"Do shorter pages convert less?”

I helped run and strategize an AB test for existing content length vs shorter content length. Our hypothesis was that less words wouldn’t hurt conversion.

Our page-views were low, so we bundled the test on 5 clients and aggregated the data.

Results:

  • Growth in conversions by 16.65%
  • No negative effect on keyword visibility. (This was found after assessing the experiment 6 months after launch)

Impact:

  • Reduced the load of what writers had to write by half!
  • UX benefits: Allows users to get their answer faster. Quicker to digest. More to-the-point.

  • Data gathering

“What CTA button converts better, 'Contact Us' or 'Get in Touch'?

On our first AB test, we sought to follow takeaways from bigger brands and test button wording. Our sites have the button wording vary from about 10 different options.

We ran the estimates through the statistical significance calculator telling us we would need a pretty big boost in conversions to make this test useful.

Results:

We didn’t get it. Optimize went to its timing limit of 3 months over 3,471 Sessions and found no leader. Although inconclusive as a whole, the opposite of our hypothesis was leading the way — “Contact Us” did better than “Get in Touch.”

Click Tracking

When Analytics data isn’t enough, you we collected engagement stats from specific triggers.

Measuring quicklink conversion rates

These big buttons send folks to a service page as opposed to a form. They seem like a good idea, but we wanted to find out if that was true.

We tracked who clicked the button then filled out a form.

Our data set included 2,751 form leads from 10 sites.

Results:

  • Average conversion rate: 14.09% (not bad compared to industry-standards).
  • This accounted for 20.78% of all form leads and 4.83% of all leads. It proved safe to keep using them as we were.
  • Established a baseline for other click-tracking-conversion experiments.

Heatmaps

These are a weak data-type, but I still think there's value to peeking behind the curtain of how your site is being used.

An aid to Conversion Analysis

We had an unhappy client who was convinced their site was under-performing.

We had no data that suggested there is a serious problem with conversion, since organic Cost Per Lead was strong. We performed this analysis in light of the client’s concerns about the drop in leads.

Overall, the analysis showed that their site was overdue for a redesign. It was outdated, too busy, and broke current internal best practice

To support these claims heatmaps proved:

  • Poor design choices. Users didn’t always know what is a link and what’s not
  • Excessive items in the nav. One had very low engagement that could be de-emphasized.
  • Little engagement on one of the two main CTA buttons. This supported a best practice of having only one.

Results:

Heatmaps helped visualize why they were in need for a redesign. They ended up pulling the trigger on one.
