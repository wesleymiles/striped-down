4 stories of contributing, guiding, and shipping projects.

1. Early Voice & Contributor to Pattern Library for Marketing Agency

Situation:

  • The company built sites mostly from scratch. Devs and designers use their own methods of reusing code and design.
  • Designers delivered desktop mockups only, so devs were responsible for all breakdowns. Because many design decisions were made by non-designers, the mobile ux was subpar.

Action:

  • I was excited about Atomic Design and pitched it to the tech team to improve our reusing methods.
  • It didn't catch on, but the spark surfaced a need to unify the names of every site piece. I joined a team that created a company-wide glossary.
  • A year later, the company was ready to implement. My team and I designed three responsive views for each piece of functionality, based on existing sites.
  • Since I understand front-end code, I acted as the conduit to development during build.

Result

  • A year later we had a working library of 60 patterns for dev and design to leverage reusing work
  • A process for library maintenance
  • Improved dev efficiently from 10 to 5 days, and design from 4 to 3 days
  • Our site’s mobile experience improved because the patterns included every responsive view.

Lesson learned: Ideas turn into realities much faster when you have top-down approval.

2. Improving Outated Landing Page Designs

Summary:

Situation:

  1. Many of our 300 clients use landing pages to drive traffic from various marketing channels. They all use a single template which was outdated, not optimized for conversion, and manual to launch.
  2. Our company prioritized improving this system. The goal was to have pages designed and built with specific purposes in mind aligned with Vertical / Segment / Service data.
  3. I was the designer on the research team converting our findings into visuals.

Action:

  1. Our research team listened to customer calls to determine the buyer personas that were common.
  2. Together, we created four personas and created four types of landing page user stories.

  3. Based on our findings, I designed a low-fi wireframe that satisfied our personas and the dynamic features the system was receiving. I leaned on internal and industry best practices for landing pages.

    For example, someone with a plumbing emergency needs to know the company can fix the problem fast, as opposed to someone considering a new heating system, who may want to learn a lot about the system and company installing it.

  4. In response to feedback from stakeholders, I designed higher fidelity wireframes.
  5. Once approved, the design team and I retrofit existing clients to the new system and created processes to get new clients up and running.

Result: 

  1. New landing page system launched.
    1. It populates with dynamic content from our dashboard and styles specified from designers.
    2. Hundreds can be created at a time now.
    3. Once the styles are added in from design, future landing page designs will have a base to pull from. It's a version of a living style guide.

Lesson: This project was a challenge because no one person owned it. Thus there was no authority to go for questions. It was a learning experience for the company.
